- Statistical Note: Exploratory vs Confirmatory Hypothesis testing  [[doc_has.comparative]]
  - exploratory  testing: new hypotheses are generated and tested in open-ended scenarios.
    Ex: Simulating rare disease presentations with synthetic data.
  - confirmatory testing: predefined hypotheses are validated with controlled experiments.
    Ex: A/B testing of diagnostic agent accuracy.

[[{qa.dont's]]
## AGENT EVALUATION THROUGH TESTEABLE HYPOTHESIS DONT's


1. Avoid Vague or Untestable Requirements
   - Bad  Example: "The AI should improve customer experience."
   - Good Example: "The AI chatbot must resolve 80% of customer queries without human 
     intervention, measured via monthly customer satisfaction surveys."
 
2. Don’t Ignore Data Bias or Distribution Shift
   - Finance Example: Training a fraud detection model only on transactions from
     one geographic region may fail to generalize globally.
   - Medical Diagnosis: Training a diagnostic AI on images from only one type 
     of camera or skin tone may reduce accuracy for underrepresented groups.
 
3. Don’t Overlook Regulatory and Ethical Constraints
  - Finance Example: Failing to log AI decisions for audits can lead to compliance
    violations.
  - Medical Diagnosis: Not anonymizing patient data in training sets violates HIPAA/GDPR.
 
4. Don’t Assume Static Requirements

  - Finance Example: Fraud patterns evolve; requirements must include regular model updates.
  - Medical Diagnosis: New diseases or variants (e.g., COVID-19) require 
    continuous retraining and validation.
 
5. Don’t Neglect Stakeholder Alignment
  - Finance Example: IT, compliance, and business teams must agree on what 
    constitutes a "false positive" and its acceptable rate.
  - Medical Diagnosis Example: Clinicians, patients, and regulators must align 
    on the trade-offs between sensitivity and specificity.

6. Over-Reliance on PROXY METRICS that do not map to real-world outcomes leading
   to misaligned incentives and suboptimal agentic AI performance.
   ```
   | Proxy Metric Pitfall | Risk   [[PM.risk]]                           |
   |----------------------|----------------------------------------------|
   | Engagement metrics   | Ignoring compliance risks                    |
   | Speed of resolution  | Degrading quality and customer satisfaction  |
   ```

7. Forget to test Failure modes or adversarial input ("prompt attacks")

8. Forget to tests for rare events. Examples:
  - financial : trading-agents untested edge cases can lead to flash crashes.
  - healthcare: unvalidated-diagnostic agents can cause misdiagnoses.

[[qa.dont's}]]

```
|                        SUMMARY: BEST PATTERNS VS. DON'TS
|
| Dimension             | Best Patterns                                        | Critical DON’Ts                                      |
|-----------------------|------------------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------------------|
| Goal Translation      | Quantify vague goals into measurable criteria        | Over-rely on proxy metrics that don’t map to outcomes|
| Hypothesis Design     | Isolate variables; avoid correlation ≠ causation     | Ignore failure modes and adversarial inputs          |
| Testing Approach      | Balance exploratory, confirmatory, and HITL testing  | Static requirements that don’t adapt to change       |
| Iterative Refinement  | Update hypotheses and test sets based on drift       | Accumulate evaluation debt by skipping stress tests  |
| Stakeholder Alignment | Align engineers, experts, compliance on metrics      | Misaligned incentives leading to gaming the system   |
```
